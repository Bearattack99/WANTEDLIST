Discovering one's identity is a complex process that requires significant time and introspection. For many individuals, the question of "Who am I?" has been a lifelong pursuit. To assist in this journey of self-discovery, various forms of media leverage their popularity to address the challenges associated with identity exploration and the difficulty of acceptance, as wells faced by those who identify as different.

“We are not what other people say we are. We are  who we know ourselves to be.”

  • Laverne Cox

Taika [a]Waititi's Jojo Rabbit serves as a satirical examination of the complexities surrounding identity formation, particularly in the context of nationalist indoctrination. By employing a blend of absurdist humor and captivating drama, Waititi sheds light on the malleability of identity under a totalitarian regime, juxtaposing youthful naivety with the harsh realities of oppressive ideological extremes. Similarly, Luka Lesson's work examines the theme of identity, reflecting on his cultural heritage and his journey toward understanding his sense of self. Luka Lesson is a contemporary Australian slam poet known for his powerful exploration of complicated themes, particularly personal identity. His works, notably Moment to Moment and Please Resist Me, reflect a deep engagement with cultural heritage, personal experience, and social justice, a nuanced dialogue around what shapes identity in a multicultural context. Both pieces of media present characters who, despite outside pressure, come out on top, living true to themselves instead of succumbing to the societal norms forced upon them. Identity’s malleability in the face of societal pressures is a persistent human trait throughout history. Throughout Taika Waititi’s Jojo Rabbit; the poems Moment to Moment and Please Resist Me of Luka Lesson; and in society today, characters are forced to battle external pressures that challenge and suppress their true identity and values, and discover that one’s identity is not determined by society.

Historically, society has faced significant challenges regarding the social acceptance of individuals whose identities differ from the norm. Jojo Rabbit illustrates this expertly through the character of Captain Klenzendorf, a closeted gay man compelled to conceal his true identity in a world that criminalises it. The film makes it clear from the very beginning that Klezendorf is hiding something, illustrating his growing struggle to keep his sexual identity secret from others. Likewise, the piece Moment to Moment by Luka Lesson, explores Luka’s fear of self-revelation. Like Klenzendorf, Luka felt the necessity to alter his identity, distancing himself from his true self to conform to the expectations set by those around him, stating, “I learned this while I was trying to dig myself out of a hole and bury the old me at the same time.”. Both Klenzendorf and Luka eventually are able to move on and be themselves, in two distinct ways. Ultimately, both Klenzendorf and Luka find ways to embrace their authentic selves, though through different paths. Nearing the end of the film, the decline of the Nazi regime marks the end for Captain Klenzendorf; it is under this pressure that he discovers the courage to dismiss societal rejection and embrace his true self, branding the pink triangle on his uniform as a symbol of pride. Lesson, on the other hand, finds sanctity in his cultural beliefs. At age 28, he reflects on his discovery of an “owl”, a symbol of wisdom, that he came to rely on for support. The owl is a symbol of Lady Athena, Greek goddess of wisdom. His encounter with her signifies his pivotal self-realisation that the perception of him others hold is of little significance compared to his own self-understanding. These cases accurately reflect the experiences of many individuals in today’s society. Each day, thousands of LGBTQIA+ individuals navigate the challenging landscape between their authentic selves and the expectations of society. Shamefully, many are left with no choice due to potential legal ramifications. Currently 64 countries worldwide criminalise homosexuality, forcing millions to suppress their true identities in order to maintain their freedom.

The aspiration for self-improvement, coupled with concerns about potential backlash, is a common experience that many individuals can relate to. Both pieces strongly highlight how external pressures, including violent indoctrination and internal conflicts, can compel individuals to abandon an old identity in search of one more resonant with themselves. This indoctrination is effectively illustrated through the portrayal of the Hitler Youth Camp in Jojo Rabbit. From his first day, Jojo exhibits signs of discomfort as he increasingly grapples with conforming to the expectations. The sinister, violent training is juxtaposed against the upbeat, almost joyous music, masking the distorted reality that is Jojo's childhood. Additionally, the knife is given to him as a weapon to defeat the enemy, though it frequently appears being taken away from him, symbolising the defeat of the true enemy, the regime. The stark contrast between the propaganda fed to them versus the true horrors of war create a false identity the nation lives in. Moment to Moment recounts a similar journey to that of Jojo, Luka’s struggle to move on. He indicates a strong desire to embrace a new beginning and transform his identity, stating his intention to "sell everything I had" to move away from his previous self. He draws inspiration from his Greek heritage, referencing the act of paying the ferryman Charon to cross the river Styx, symbolising a process of death and rebirth. Ultimately, Luka, like Jojo, is able to move past his fear of being different, and start on the journey to be who he wants to be. These journeys mirror that of millions of individuals each day in the real world. In communities worldwide, individuals often navigate the tension between their desire for change and the scrutiny and criticism that can follow. The malleability of identity under pressure can lead to more frequent shifts in personal identity; however, the acceptance of others' transformations remains challenging for many, and needs to change.

To foster personal growth, it is often beneficial to establish a support network of trustworthy and caring individuals to have around you. For Jojo, this person is Elsa, a jewish girl Jojo’s mother took in during the war. Initially resistant to her presence, Jojo gradually begins to understand that Elsa is the only person in his life who truly accepts him for who he is. While others belittle him for his ‘hideous scar’, Elsa provides him with unwavering support and compassion, treating him like a brother, regardless of his identity. The understanding that Elsa is not an opponent of Jojo is a crucial turning point for his character, allowing him to progress and live for himself, disregarding expectations of the regime. Lesson’s discovery of the owl symbolising Lady Athena serves the same role as Jojo’s of Elsa. Her guidance allows him to understand there’s no reason to feel guilty for wanting to change, reflected in Please Resist Me, where he says “I live like a citizen, you live a life like imprisonment”. This line sends a powerful message, that the only way to truly live life is to move past the chains of others opinions, and to live true to oneself. Finding a soul who wholeheartedly embraces you for who you are eases the tribulations on the journey of personal growth and finding oneself. For many LGBTQIA+ individuals, this empowers them to break free from the stifling chains of societal pressure, allowing their authentic selves to shine.

Through the illustration of the struggles of identity, both internal and external, modern media such as Jojo Rabbit and the verse of Luka Lesson continue to explore the human condition, prompting the audience to tackle difficult questions. Why do we find it challenging to accept one another unless we all conform to the same standards? Despite their distinct contexts, both works address significant global issues in an accessible yet effective way, paving the way for a more inclusive and accepting world.
